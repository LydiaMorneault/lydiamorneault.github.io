Master's Thesis:
Incremental Evaluation of Instrument Recognition Data

A framework for making the process of annotating audio data more efficient.

Music datasets are notoriously difficult to construct and even more difficult to annotate. With a problem like instrument recognition, not only do annotators have to take time to listen to each piece of audio, they must label the presence of multiple instruments at once. This process can be incredibly inefficient and costly to the researchers and institutions maintaining these datasets. The significance of having well-labeled, open-source datasets is immense for the music and audio research community, as they provide unrestricted accessibility to anyone interested in using them. This leads to further development, progression, and innovation in the field of music information retrieval. This project focuses on the development and implementation of an incremental evaluation system for the instrument recognition dataset, OpenMIC-2018. The OpenMIC dataset contains a large number of audio tracks across 20 instrument classes, but only a small fraction of these tracks have been labeled. The incremental evaluation system uses the process of algorithmic disagreement to prioritize the unlabeled data so that the most useful tracks are put at the top of the annotation queue. This system achieved a 7% increase in efficiency on average across all classes, meaning that a model trained on a fraction of the data can achieve the same accuracy as a model trained on all of the data. In some cases, the partially-annotated model achieved an even higher accuracy than the fully-annotated model.

The significance of this project lies in its potential to reduce the costs associated with creating high-quality datasets for instrument recognition. The development of this system could pave the way for similar approaches to be applied to other problems in MIR, such as chord recognition. Overall, this project serves as an important step towards more efficient and cost-effective methods of creating and evaluating music datasets.
